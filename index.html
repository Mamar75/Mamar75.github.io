  • Indetermincay: The Ultimate Limit to Arbitrage – Publicly traded companies sometimes own shares in other publicly traded companies. We refer to the owning company as the \emph{parent} and the held company as the \emph{subsidiary}. According to a basic \emph{consistency} principle, if the value of a parent company's stake in a subsidiary increases by~\$1 million, the parent company's market value should also increase by~\$1 million, assuming the rest of the parent company's assets and liabilities remain constant. Although the market value of the parent company’s other assets and liabilities is not directly observable, it likely correlates with the subsidiary’s market value. Therefore, it is crucial to account for common movements unrelated to ownership. Assuming these can be captured by closely related stock price movements, I found that, among a panel of parent--subsidiary pairs, on average, a~\$1 million weekly increase (or decrease) in the value of a parent company’s stake in a subsidiary results in only a~\$550{,}000 weekly increase (or decrease) in the parent company’s market value. This suggests that consistency does not hold. The presence of the parent’s other assets limits arbitrage, as it becomes difficult to verify in real time whether consistency holds, and arbitrage positions cannot be perfectly hedged. However, when no other assets are present, the transmission rate is indistinguishable from one.
